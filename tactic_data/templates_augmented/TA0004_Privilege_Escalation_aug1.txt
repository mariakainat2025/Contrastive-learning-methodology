======================================================================
  MITRE ATT&CK — TACTIC: TA0004
======================================================================
  ID         : TA0004
  NAME       : Privilege Escalation

  OBJECTIVE  : The adversary is attempting to obtain higher-level permissions.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
  TECHNIQUES USE
----------------------------------------------------------------------

  Privilege Escalation includes techniques used by adversaries to
  obtain elevated permissions on a system or within a network.
  Attackers may initially gain limited access but require higher
  privileges to achieve their objectives. Common methods include
  exploiting system vulnerabilities, misconfigurations, and design
  weaknesses. Examples of elevated access include SYSTEM/root level,
  local administrator rights, or user accounts with administrative
  or elevated functional privileges.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
  OUTCOME / TACTIC LINKS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

  These techniques often overlap with Persistence techniques, since
  system features that provide persistence may also execute with
  elevated privileges.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
  LINUX DETECTION STRATEGIES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

  Monitor audit logs for setuid/setgid modifications, cases where UID
  differs from EUID (indicating sudo or privilege escalation), and
  execution of high-privilege binaries by unprivileged users.

  Use of native utilities or scripts (e.g., `usermod`, `passwd`,
  `groupmod`) to elevate permissions or modify user privileges,
  correlated with login or process activity.

  Correlation of systemd service creation or /etc/init.d script
  changes with abnormal process execution during system startup.

  Detection of modifications or execution of shell startup scripts
  such as .bashrc, .profile, or /etc/profile used for persistence or
  privilege-related behavior.

  Detects creation or modification of systemd service units,
  scheduling of cron jobs executed at boot, or writes to
  /etc/init.d/ with chmod +x or systemctl usage from non-root users.

  Detection of Linux container escape attempts via syscalls such as
  unshare, keyctl, or mount, where processes break out of container
  isolation into the host namespace.

  Detects changes in auditd or inotify rules monitoring system files
  combined with execution of scripts or binaries triggered by cron or
  systemd timers.

  Detects exploitation of vulnerable setuid binaries or kernel
  modules, often combined with suspicious access to /proc/kallsyms or
  /dev/kmem.

  Detects ptrace or memfd-based process injection via syscall
  monitoring (ptrace, mmap), especially when targeting active
  processes with unusual memory writes or file descriptors.

  Detects creation or modification of cron jobs or systemd timer
  units executed by unusual users or outside expected schedules.

  Detection of valid account abuse via SSH logins, sudo/su misuse,
  and service account anomalies outside expected baselines.
