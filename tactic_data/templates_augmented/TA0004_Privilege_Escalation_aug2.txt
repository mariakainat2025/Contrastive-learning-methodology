======================================================================
  MITRE ATT&CK — TACTIC: TA0004
======================================================================
  ID         : TA0004
  NAME       : Privilege Escalation

  OBJECTIVE  : The adversary seeks to gain elevated permissions.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
  TECHNIQUES USE
----------------------------------------------------------------------

  Privilege Escalation refers to techniques used by attackers to
  acquire higher-level permissions on a system or network. While
  initial access may be limited, adversaries often require elevated
  privileges to continue their operations. Common techniques include
  exploitation of vulnerabilities, system misconfigurations, and
  design weaknesses. Elevated access may include SYSTEM/root level,
  local administrator rights, or privileged user accounts with
  administrative capabilities.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
  OUTCOME / TACTIC LINKS
----------------------------------------------------------------------

  These techniques frequently overlap with Persistence, as mechanisms
  used to maintain access may also execute with elevated privileges.

----------------------------------------------------------------------
  LINUX DETECTION STRATEGIES
----------------------------------------------------------------------

  Monitor audit logs for setuid/setgid changes, mismatches between UID
  and EUID (indicating privilege escalation via sudo), and execution
  of privileged binaries by non-privileged users.

  Use of system utilities or scripts (e.g., `usermod`, `passwd`,
  `groupmod`) to increase user privileges, correlated with login and
  process activity.

  Correlation between systemd service modifications or /etc/init.d
  changes and abnormal execution behavior during boot time.

  Detection of modifications or execution of shell startup files such
  as .bashrc, .profile, or /etc/profile that may influence elevated
  execution behavior.

  Detects creation or modification of systemd units, boot-time cron
  jobs, or writes to /etc/init.d/, including permission changes and
  systemctl execution from non-root processes.

  Detection of container escape attempts using syscalls like unshare,
  keyctl, or mount, where processes break out of isolated namespaces
  into the host environment.

  Detects auditd/inotify rule changes that monitor system files,
  combined with execution triggered via cron or systemd timers.

  Detects exploitation of setuid binaries or kernel-level
  vulnerabilities, often paired with access to /proc/kallsyms or
  /dev/kmem.

  Detects ptrace or memfd-based injection using syscall telemetry
  (ptrace, mmap), particularly when targeting active processes.

  Detects creation or modification of cron or systemd timer jobs
  executed by unusual users or outside normal execution windows.

  Detection of valid account misuse via SSH, sudo/su abuse, and
  service account anomalies outside expected behavior patterns.
